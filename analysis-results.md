## Quality

### Finding 1: Legacy Machinery Blocks Compliance—Selective Modernization Proves Viability

**Overview:**
Ghana's textile base is dominated by 50–60-year-old equipment, while global T&G technology refreshes every 5–10 years. This structural obsolescence makes compliance certifications (required by export buyers) prohibitively expensive for individual firms. However, selective line retooling has already demonstrated productivity and quality gains at Juapong Textiles, proving that targeted capex can unlock export readiness. The mechanism is straightforward: outdated machinery cannot meet buyer specs; modernization is capital-intensive but proven to work. This directly shapes park design (shared QA labs, pooled capex financing) and tenant selection (prioritize operators with modernization track records).

**Evidence:**
- "Legacy textile assets judged non-revivable. Machinery is 50–60 years old" (Munish Gupta, ARISE).
- "Selective modernization already proved productivity gains. Prior private partners retooled parts of the line to improve quality and efficiency" (Jerome, Juapong Textiles).

**Commercial implication:** Export certifications are gating items; individual firms cannot absorb certification costs alone. Pooled compliance infrastructure (shared labs, bulk audits) reduces per-unit cost and accelerates buyer qualification.

**Decision implication:** Park design must include shared QA/testing facilities. Tenant selection should prioritize operators with capex appetite and modernization experience. Site sensitivity: Tema and Bright International Zone already host modernized lines; Oxygen City will need to attract or co-invest with capex-ready operators.

## Cost

### Finding 1: Electricity Cost Gap ($0.12/kWh vs. $0.05/kWh Target) Is the Binding Constraint

**Overview:**
Current electricity costs (~$0.12/kWh) are 2.4× the draft textile policy target ($0.05/kWh). This is the single largest structural cost driver and the primary reason AGOA-era competitiveness has eroded. Operators cannot compete on cost with Asian suppliers unless power costs fall. The mechanism is direct: T&G is energy-intensive; high power costs translate to uncompetitive landed costs. This is not a perception issue—it is a structural economics problem that shapes site selection, park viability, and investor targeting.

**Evidence:**
- "Energy economics drive viability. Draft textile policy target of $0.05/kWh electricity versus observed current level around $0.12/kWh" (Munish Gupta, ARISE).
- "Energy cost is the binding utilities issue. Electricity and utilities are a key frustration" (Janet Abobigu, UNI-JAY).

**Commercial implication:** Without subsidized or on-site power (solar, gas), operators cannot achieve export-competitive margins. Pooled utilities (shared power infrastructure) become a critical park amenity.

**Decision implication:** Park viability depends on power cost reduction. Prioritize sites with access to subsidized power (e.g., Tema industrial tariffs, Akosombo hydro proximity). Investor targeting should emphasize power cost roadmap and on-site generation potential. Site sensitivity: Akosombo Industrial Limited's steam generation costs are high; Oxygen City's proximity to Togo border may enable cross-border power sourcing.

### Finding 2: Customs Fee Fragmentation Raises Input Costs—Structural Policy Fix Needed

**Overview:**
Ghana's customs system charges per bill of entry, fragmenting import costs. When a single shipment includes multiple input types (yarn, dyes, chemicals), operators pay multiple customs fees instead of one consolidated fee. This is a policy-level inefficiency that raises input costs structurally. The mechanism is administrative: fragmented billing = higher per-unit customs burden. This is not a park-level issue; it requires government policy reform. However, it directly impacts operator margins and site competitiveness.

**Evidence:**
- "Fragmented customs fee structure raises input costs. When imports include multiple bills of entry, Ghana charges per bill" (Wasantha Perera, Dignity DTRT).

**Commercial implication:** Operators absorb higher input costs; margins compress. Free Zone status (if available) can mitigate this by reducing customs friction.

**Decision implication:** Advocate for customs consolidation policy reform. Prioritize sites with Free Zone status or customs exemptions (e.g., Bright International Zone). Include customs facilitation in park operating model.

## Market

### Finding 1: AGOA Advantage Has Eroded—Cost Competitiveness, Not Preferential Access, Now Drives Buyer Selection

**Overview:**
Operators previously leveraged AGOA preferential tariffs to win US orders. However, cost competitiveness has deteriorated, and AGOA advantage is no longer sufficient to overcome price gaps versus Asian suppliers. The mechanism is straightforward: preferential tariffs cannot offset structural cost disadvantages (power, inputs, compliance). This signals a strategic shift: Ghana's T&G positioning must move from tariff-arbitrage to quality/compliance differentiation or cost reduction. Current buyer bases remain US-concentrated (legacy AGOA relationships), but operators are explicitly pushing into Europe to diversify away from AGOA dependence.

**Evidence:**
- "AGOA advantage faded. They previously used AGOA-related benefits to win orders, but now they cannot compete on cost" (Wasantha Perera, Dignity DTRT).
- "Client base: current US concentration, deliberate Europe push. Clients remain predominantly US-based, explicitly linked to AGOA" (Wasantha Perera, Dignity DTRT).

**Commercial implication:** Operators must compete on cost or quality, not tariffs. Export strategy must shift toward EU compliance (REACH, labor standards) and quality differentiation. Domestic procurement (uniforms, workwear) becomes a more stable revenue stream than export.

**Decision implication:** Park positioning should emphasize cost reduction (power, logistics) and compliance infrastructure (QA labs, certifications). Investor targeting should focus on operators with EU buyer relationships or domestic procurement contracts. Site sensitivity: Tema's port proximity supports export logistics; Oxygen City's border location supports regional sourcing and ECOWAS trade.

## Access

### Finding 1: Import Dependence Locks in High Input Costs—Backward Integration Opportunity

**Overview:**
Ghana imports core T&G inputs (cotton, dyes, chemicals), creating structural input cost disadvantages. This is not a logistics issue; it is a supply-chain architecture problem. The mechanism is straightforward: imported inputs carry freight, tariffs, and working capital costs that domestic suppliers would not. This directly impacts operator margins and competitiveness. However, it also signals a backward integration opportunity: if a park can attract or develop input suppliers (dye houses, chemical blending, yarn spinning), it can reduce input costs and create cluster effects.

**Evidence:**
- "Import dependence keeps input costs structurally high. Ghana imports core inputs such as cotton, dyes, and ink" (Edwina Assan, Association of Ghana Industries).

**Commercial implication:** Operators absorb higher input costs; margins compress. Backward integration (on-site dye houses, chemical suppliers) can reduce costs and increase reliability.

**Decision implication:** Park design should include space for input suppliers and support services. Investor targeting should include dye houses, chemical suppliers, and yarn spinners. Site sensitivity: Tema's port proximity supports input imports; Akosombo's proximity to chemical suppliers (Tema) supports backward integration.

### Finding 2: Finance System Collapse Destroyed Cotton Production Model—Structural Barrier to Backward Integration

**Overview:**
Ghana's historical cotton production relied on structured pre-financing (land prep, inputs, chemicals, aggregation). This system has collapsed, leaving no institutional mechanism to finance cotton production. This is a structural barrier to backward integration and domestic input supply. The mechanism is institutional: without pre-financing, smallholder cotton farmers cannot invest in production; without domestic cotton, T&G operators remain import-dependent. This is a policy-level issue that requires government or development finance intervention.

**Evidence:**
- "Financing Breakdown Destroyed the Production Model. The historical cotton system relied on structured pre-financing of land preparation, inputs, chemicals, and aggregation" (James Wiyor, Cotton Sector Stakeholder).

**Gap:** No data on current cotton production volumes, financing gaps, or potential for revival. Next step: assess cotton sector financing needs and potential for development finance intervention.
