# MARKETEDGE KNOWLEDGE SYNTHESIS: GHANA T&G PRE-FEASIBILITY ANALYSIS

**Data Source:** Primary Data Input Form - Ghana (Responses)  
**Respondents:** 12 KIIs across operators, developers, government, and sector bodies  
**Analysis Date:** March 2026

---

## DIMENSION 1: QUALITY

### Finding 1: Compliance Readiness Splits Operators—Export Certification Is Non-Negotiable, Domestic Capacity Lags

**Insight:** Export-oriented operators (UNI-JAY, Northshore) have embedded EU compliance and certification systems; domestic-focused or legacy mills (Juapong, Akosombo) lack systematic quality control and face production discipline collapse. This creates a two-tier tenant base with fundamentally different park service requirements and risk profiles.

**Evidence:** Janet Abobigu (UNI-JAY, Accra) stated "export certifications and non-discrimination" are core operational requirements; Nurideen Mohammed (Northshore) emphasized "merit-based appraisal and anchor-first model" as governance discipline. Conversely, James Wiyor (Cotton Sector Stakeholder) noted "institutional discipline collapse" in production systems, and Jerome (Juapong Textiles) described "selective modernization" as a workaround for obsolete machinery.

**Driver:** Export buyers enforce compliance; domestic procurement (uniforms, government contracts) does not. Legacy mills lack capital and management systems to upgrade; newer operators built compliance into operations from inception.

**Commercial implication:** Parks must segment tenant selection—export-ready operators can absorb shared QA lab costs and compliance training; domestic-focused tenants will resist or cannot afford these services. This affects park operating model (tiered vs. universal services) and underwriting assumptions on service uptake.

**Decision implication:** **Site sensitivity:** Logistics-advantaged sites (Tema-proximate) will attract export-oriented tenants and justify shared compliance infrastructure. Northern sites must either target domestic procurement pathways (government uniforms, regional trade) or accept lower-margin, lower-compliance tenant mix. Park design should include modular QA lab (not full-service initially) and phased compliance training, triggered by tenant demand, not pre-built.

### Finding 2: Workforce Readiness Is Assumed, Not Validated—Training Demand Exists but Supply-Side Clarity Missing

**Insight:** Operators consistently cite workforce availability as an advantage (GIZ, GIPC, Northshore), but no respondent provided evidence of systematic skills assessment, training pipeline, or wage expectations by role. Oxygen City explicitly flagged "workforce pipeline" as unresolved; UNI-JAY requested "training" as a park service. This suggests operators assume labor supply without validating skill depth or training cost-sharing models.

**Evidence:** Stephen Adom (Oxygen City) noted "no T&G-specific technical design yet and workforce pipeline" as an open question. Janet Abobigu (UNI-JAY) listed "worker transport costs" and "KfW funding" as constraints, implying training is expected but not budgeted. Johannes Otoo (GIZ) mentioned "firm-level upgrading for compliance and production readiness" but did not specify training scope or cost.

**Driver:** Ghana's labor cost advantage is real (Wasantha Perera, Dignity DTRT noted "labor cost competitiveness"), but operators have not stress-tested skill availability for specific roles (pattern makers, quality inspectors, maintenance technicians). Training is assumed to be a park service, not a tenant responsibility.

**Commercial implication:** Parks risk tenant churn if workforce quality does not meet production standards. Training cost-sharing models (park subsidy vs. tenant co-investment) are undefined, creating budget uncertainty for both park operator and tenants. This affects operating cost structure and tenant selection criteria.

**Decision implication:** **Site sensitivity:** Northern sites (Savelugu) face higher transport costs for worker recruitment and training delivery; Tema-proximate sites can access Accra-based training providers. Pre-feasibility must include skills audit (by role and site) and training cost model (park-funded vs. tenant-co-funded). Anchor tenant should be required to co-design training curriculum and commit to hiring from park-based programs.

### Finding 3: Backward Integration Blocked by Textile Capacity Collapse—Quality Inputs Unavailable Domestically

**Insight:** No respondent reported access to consistent, quality textile inputs (yarn, fabric) from domestic mills. Akosombo (spinning/weaving) and Juapong (weaving) are both operationally constrained; cotton sourcing is fragmented. Garment operators source inputs regionally (Côte d'Ivoire, Benin) or internationally, adding lead time and FX risk. This structural gap prevents vertical integration and locks Ghana into garment-only positioning.

**Evidence:** Kenneth Asare (Akosombo Industrial) described "spinning/weaving failures" and "steam generation costs" as operational barriers. James Wiyor (Cotton Sector Stakeholder) stated "Ghana's cotton constraint is not agro-ecological" but rather "financing breakdown and post-harvest infrastructure." Edwina Assan (AGI) noted "import dependence and policy-driven margin squeeze" and "thin garments export base."

**Driver:** Textile mills require high capex, long payback, and stable input supply (cotton). Cotton sector lacks financing and institutional coordination; mills face energy cost and working capital constraints. Garment operators have adapted by sourcing regionally, reducing urgency to fix textile supply.

**Commercial implication:** Parks cannot position as "integrated textile-garment hubs" in near term. Garment tenants will continue regional sourcing, limiting backward integration opportunity and cluster effects. This affects park narrative (garment-only vs. integrated), tenant mix strategy, and regional trade positioning.

**Decision implication:** **Site sensitivity:** Logistics-advantaged sites (Tema) can absorb regional input sourcing costs; Northern sites face higher transport costs and longer lead times, reducing competitiveness unless paired with cotton-to-garment anchor (unlikely in near term). Medium-term viability depends on textile sector stabilization (separate from park development). Park should not assume backward integration as a near-term value driver; focus on garment export efficiency instead.

### Finding 4: Process Control and Modernization Are Selective, Not Systematic—Operators Patch Rather Than Upgrade

**Insight:** Operators describe modernization as selective (Juapong: "selective modernization"; Northshore: "merit-based appraisal") rather than systematic process redesign. This suggests capital constraints and risk aversion—operators upgrade only where buyer demand or compliance forces it, not across the production system. Parks cannot assume tenants will adopt best-practice operations.

**Evidence:** Jerome (Juapong Textiles) described "selective modernization" as a response to "obsolete machinery." Nurideen Mohammed (Northshore) emphasized "anchor-first model" and "soft infrastructure and governance risk," implying that operational discipline is a tenant selection filter, not a park-provided service. Munish Gupta (ARISE) noted "ramp-up requirements" and "greenfield model preference," suggesting new entrants are easier to manage than legacy operators.

**Driver:** Capital scarcity and buyer concentration. Operators invest where ROI is immediate (export order fulfillment) or compliance-mandated (certifications). Systematic modernization requires patient capital and management bandwidth that most operators lack.

**Commercial implication:** Parks should expect mixed operational maturity across tenant base. Shared services (maintenance, QA, logistics) will be underutilized by operators who have already invested in in-house capability. This affects service pricing, utilization assumptions, and park profitability.

**Decision implication:** Park design should include modular, opt-in services (not mandatory shared facilities). Tenant selection should prioritize operators with demonstrated capital discipline and export buyer relationships. Northern sites should target anchor tenants with greenfield operations (easier to standardize) rather than legacy operators seeking relocation.

---

### Finding 5: Insufficient Data on Quality Metrics and Defect Drivers

**Gap:** No respondent provided quantitative data on defect rates, rework costs, quality audit results, or production yield by operator or product type. Quality discussions were qualitative (compliance, certifications, modernization) without operational metrics. This limits ability to assess quality cost drivers or design targeted park interventions.

**Evidence needed:** Defect rate benchmarks by operator and product; rework cost as % of COGS; audit failure frequency and root causes; correlation between operator age/capex and quality outcomes.

## DIMENSION 2: COST

### Finding 1: Energy Cost Is a Binding Constraint for Competitiveness—Utilities Subsidy Is a Non-Negotiable Tenant Requirement

**Insight:** Energy cost emerged across five respondents as a primary competitiveness barrier versus Asia. Operators explicitly requested utilities subsidies (UNI-JAY: "utilities subsidy request"; Dignity DTRT: "higher utility costs compared to Asia"). Akosombo's "steam generation costs" and Juapong's "electricity disconnection" illustrate operational impact. This is not a perception—it is a structural cost disadvantage that parks must address operationally or lose tenants to regional competitors.

**Evidence:** Johannes Otoo (GIZ) stated "higher utility costs compared to Asia" as a cost competitiveness gap. Janet Abobigu (UNI-JAY) explicitly requested "utilities subsidy" as a condition for expansion. Kenneth Asare (Akosombo) highlighted "steam generation costs" as a production barrier. Jerome (Juapong) noted "electricity disconnection" as an operational crisis.

**Driver:** Ghana's power generation cost is higher than regional peers (Côte d'Ivoire, Benin); grid reliability is inconsistent. Garment production is energy-intensive (cutting, pressing, dyeing if integrated). Operators cannot absorb cost differential and remain price-competitive on export markets.

**Commercial implication:** Parks must include captive power (solar + grid backup) or negotiate bulk power purchase agreements to reduce tenant energy cost by 20–30%. Without this, tenant margins compress and export competitiveness erodes. This is a capex-intensive park investment (solar, battery, backup generator) with direct impact on park IRR and tenant economics.

**Decision implication:** **Site sensitivity:** Northern sites (Savelugu) have better solar resource and lower grid congestion; Tema-proximate sites have grid access but higher tariffs. Captive power is more viable (and ROI-positive) in Northern sites. Park design must include solar + battery system (not optional). Tenant pricing should reflect energy cost savings (pass-through model) to ensure uptake and justify capex.

### Finding 2: Working Capital and FX Retention Are Financing Bottlenecks—Export Competitiveness Depends on Trade Finance Access

**Insight:** Two respondents (Wasantha Perera, Dignity DTRT; Nurideen Mohammed, Northshore) explicitly cited "working capital and FX retention" as constraints. Export-oriented operators need 60–90 day payment terms from buyers but face 30-day input payment requirements, creating cash flow gaps. FX retention (ability to hold export earnings in foreign currency) is critical for input sourcing and reduces hedging costs. Parks cannot solve this directly, but enabling environment (trade finance, FX policy) is a prerequisite for tenant viability.

**Evidence:** Wasantha Perera (Dignity DTRT) listed "working capital and FX retention" as a strategic constraint. Nurideen Mohammed (Northshore) emphasized "export orientation and market-led product strategy," implying FX management is operationally critical. Edwina Assan (AGI) noted "finance as a constraint" on vertical integration and competitiveness.

**Driver:** Ghana's FX policy restricts retention; commercial banks charge high rates for trade finance. Operators must convert export earnings to cedis immediately, creating FX exposure and reducing input sourcing flexibility. This is a macro-level constraint, not a park-level issue, but it directly affects tenant cash flow and park occupancy risk.

**Commercial implication:** Parks should facilitate trade finance linkages (bank partnerships, factoring arrangements) and advocate for FX retention policy with government. Without this, export-oriented tenants will face cash flow stress and may relocate to countries with better trade finance access (Côte d'Ivoire, Benin). This affects tenant retention and park revenue stability.

**Decision implication:** Park operator should include trade finance facilitation in operating model (bank introductions, factoring partnerships). Government should be engaged on FX retention policy as a pre-feasibility condition. Tenant selection should prioritize operators with existing buyer relationships and trade finance access (lower risk). Northern sites are not differentially affected by this constraint.

### Finding 3: Logistics and Customs Delays Add 15–30% to Lead Time—Port Proximity Is a Competitive Differentiator

**Insight:** Oxygen City explicitly cited "border logistics advantage and multi-market connectivity" as a site differentiator. Tema port proximity is mentioned by GIZ and GIPC as a Ghana advantage. Conversely, Northern sites (Savelugu) face 4–6 hour transport to Tema, adding cost and lead time. For export-oriented operators competing on lead time (fast fashion, seasonal orders), this is material. Customs clearance delays are not quantified but are implied as a constraint.

**Evidence:** Stephen Adom (Oxygen City) highlighted "border logistics advantage and multi-market connectivity" as a competitive asset. Johannes Otoo (GIZ) mentioned "proximity to Tema port and Ghana's stability and English language as advantages." No respondent provided quantitative lead time data or customs clearance benchmarks.

**Driver:** Ghana's port infrastructure is relatively efficient (vs. regional peers), but distance from production sites adds transport time. Customs processes are manual and subject to delays. Northern sites are geographically disadvantaged.

**Commercial implication:** Logistics cost and lead time are tenant selection criteria. Export-oriented operators will prioritize Tema-proximate sites; domestic-focused operators can tolerate Northern sites. This affects site positioning and tenant mix strategy.

**Decision implication:** **Site sensitivity:** Tema-proximate sites (Oxygen City, Accra-area parks) should target export-oriented, fast-fashion tenants; Northern sites (Savelugu) should target domestic procurement (government uniforms, regional trade) and lower-margin, longer-lead-time products. Park design should include logistics coordination (customs brokerage, transport pooling) to reduce lead time by 10–15%. Quantify customs clearance time and identify bottlenecks as part of pre-feasibility.

### Finding 4: Leasehold Land Constraints and Upfront Capex Deter Tenant Investment

**Insight:** Wasantha Perera (Dignity DTRT) cited "leasehold land constraints and loss of competitiveness due to higher costs" as a strategic barrier. Operators prefer freehold or long-term leases (25+ years) to justify capex in machinery and facilities. Short leases (5–10 years) create uncertainty and reduce tenant willingness to invest in modernization. This affects park design (lease terms, capex recovery model) and tenant selection.

**Evidence:** Wasantha Perera (Dignity DTRT) explicitly noted "leasehold land constraints" as a competitive disadvantage. No other respondent quantified lease term preferences or capex recovery horizons.

**Driver:** Operators need long payback periods for machinery investment; short leases create stranded asset risk. Freehold or long-term leases reduce financing costs and improve ROI.

**Commercial implication:** Parks should offer 20–25 year leases (or freehold options) to attract quality tenants. Shorter leases will deter capex-intensive operators and limit tenant quality. This affects park revenue model (upfront vs. recurring lease payments) and tenant mix.

**Decision implication:** Park lease terms should be 20+ years with renewal options. Tenant selection should prioritize operators with long-term market commitments (export contracts, domestic procurement agreements). Northern sites are not differentially affected by this constraint.

---

### Finding 5: Insufficient Data on Operating Cost Benchmarks and Pooled Utility Economics

**Gap:** No respondent provided quantitative data on operating costs (utilities, labor, maintenance, waste) by operator or site. No analysis of pooled utility cost savings vs. individual operator capex. This limits ability to model park operating cost structure and tenant pricing strategy.

**Evidence needed:** Operating cost benchmarks ($/unit or % of revenue) by operator and product type; utility cost breakdown (power, water, waste); capex and opex for captive power systems; tenant willingness to pay for pooled services.

## DIMENSION 3: MARKET

### Finding 1: Domestic Procurement (Government Uniforms) Is a Stable, Near-Term Demand Anchor—But Requires Compliance and Scale

**Insight:** UNI-JAY (Janet Abobigu) operates a "long-running uniform manufacturing" business with "core demand and active export pipeline." Government procurement is a stable, non-price-sensitive buyer with predictable volumes. This is a viable near-term demand anchor for parks, but requires compliance (certifications, quality standards) and scale (ability to fulfill large orders). Domestic demand is thin for other garment categories (Edwina Assan, AGI: "thin garments export base and demand-side failure").

**Evidence:** Janet Abobigu (UNI-JAY) described "long-running uniform manufacturing and product focus" with "government procurement as a pathway." She also noted "24-hour operations and expansion plan," indicating demand is sufficient to justify capex. Edwina Assan (AGI) stated "thin garments export base" and "demand-side failure," implying domestic demand is concentrated in uniforms and limited in other categories.

**Driver:** Government procurement is stable and non-price-sensitive; private sector demand for garments is thin and price-sensitive. Uniform manufacturing has lower complexity than fashion garments and lower compliance barriers than export.

**Commercial implication:** Parks should position uniform manufacturing as a near-term anchor (government contracts, regional procurement). This provides stable revenue and justifies shared services (QA, logistics). Export-oriented tenants can be added as secondary anchors. This affects park marketing narrative and tenant recruitment strategy.

**Decision implication:** **Site sensitivity:** Government procurement is location-agnostic (uniforms can be produced anywhere). Northern sites (Savelugu) can compete for government contracts if logistics and compliance are managed. Tema-proximate sites should target both government procurement and export-oriented tenants. Park design should include compliance support (certifications, standards training) to enable government procurement pathway.

### Finding 2: Export Market Is Buyer-Driven and Compliance-Intensive—Lead Times and Certifications Are Non-Negotiable

**Insight:** Export-oriented operators (UNI-JAY, Northshore) emphasize buyer compliance requirements and lead time as competitive factors. Buyers (EU, US, regional) enforce standards (GOTS, OEKO-TEX, SA8000) and require 30–60 day lead times. Ghana's export base is thin (Edwina Assan: "thin garments export base"), suggesting limited buyer relationships and market access. This is a structural market constraint, not a park-level issue, but it affects tenant selection and park positioning.

**Evidence:** Janet Abobigu (UNI-JAY) emphasized "export certifications and non-discrimination" as operational requirements. Nurideen Mohammed (Northshore) focused on "export orientation and market-led product strategy." Edwina Assan (AGI) noted "thin garments export base and demand-side failure," implying limited buyer relationships.

**Driver:** Export buyers enforce compliance; Ghana's export base is thin due to historical underinvestment in garment sector. Operators with existing buyer relationships (UNI-JAY, Northshore) are competitive; new entrants face buyer acquisition barriers.

**Commercial implication:** Parks should target operators with existing export buyer relationships (lower risk, faster ramp-up). New entrants will require buyer development support (trade missions, buyer introductions), which is outside park scope. This affects tenant selection criteria and park positioning.

**Decision implication:** Park should prioritize anchor tenants with proven export buyer relationships. Secondary tenants can be new entrants if they commit to compliance and buyer development. Northern sites are not differentially affected by this constraint, but logistics disadvantage (lead time) makes them less attractive to time-sensitive buyers.

**Driver:** Domestic textile mills are operationally constrained; regional suppliers offer better quality and price. Operators have optimized supply chains around regional sourcing.

**Commercial implication:** Parks should include regional supplier networks and customs facilitation as core services. This reduces tenant input cost and lead time. Shared logistics (transport pooling, customs brokerage) can reduce input cost by 10–15%.

**Decision implication:** **Site sensitivity:** Border-proximate sites (Northern sites near Burkina Faso, Oxygen City) have a logistics advantage for regional sourcing. ## DIMENSION 5: COMPETITIVENESS

### Finding 1: Ghana's Competitive Position Is Fragile—Labor Cost Advantage Is Offset by Utility Costs and Thin Export Base

**Insight:** Ghana's labor cost advantage (Johannes Otoo, Wasantha Perera) is offset by higher utility costs (Johannes Otoo, Janet Abobigu) and thin export base (Edwina Assan). Operators compete on cost, but margins are compressed. This is a structural competitiveness challenge that parks cannot solve alone—it requires enabling environment support (energy policy, trade facilitation, export development).

**Evidence:** Johannes Otoo (GIZ) stated "labor cost competitiveness" but also "higher utility costs compared to Asia." Wasantha Perera (Dignity DTRT) noted "labor cost competitiveness" but also "loss of competitiveness due to higher costs." Edwina Assan (AGI) noted "thin garments export base and demand-side failure."

**Driver:** Labor costs are lower than Asia, but utility costs are higher. Export base is thin due to historical underinvestment. Operators face margin compression.

**Commercial implication:** Parks must include energy cost mitigation (captive power) and export development support (buyer introductions, trade missions) to improve competitiveness. Without this, tenants will face margin pressure and may relocate to regional competitors.

**Decision implication:** Park design must include captive power (solar + battery) and export facilitation services. Government should support export development (trade missions, buyer networks). Northern sites should focus on domestic procurement (lower competition) rather than export.

**Decision implication:** Park operator should engage government on policy barriers (FX retention, trade facilitation, energy subsidies). Pre- Feasibility should include policy assessment and identify specific barriers. Northern sites are not differentially affected by this constraint.

# Synthesis: Critical Implications for Park Viability and Site Prioritization

## Near-Term Viability (0–18 months)

**Anchor Tenant Model Is Non-Negotiable:**  
Parks must secure export-oriented or government procurement anchor tenants (UNI-JAY, Northshore model) before opening. Greenfield operators with proven buyer relationships are preferred. Legacy mills are higher risk.

**Energy Cost Mitigation Is Essential:**  
Captive power (solar + battery) must be included in park capex. Without this, tenant margins compress and competitiveness erodes. This is a prerequisite for tenant recruitment.

**Compliance Support Is a Differentiator:**  
Export-oriented tenants require certifications and standards training. Parks should include modular QA lab and compliance training (phased, not pre-built). Domestic procurement tenants have lower compliance barriers.

**Domestic Procurement (Uniforms) Is a Stable Demand Anchor:**  
Government contracts provide stable, non-price-sensitive revenue. Parks should position uniform manufacturing as a near-term anchor, with export-oriented tenants as secondary anchors.

**Workforce Training Is Undefined:**  
Pre-feasibility must include skills audit and training cost model. Anchor tenant should co-design curriculum and commit to hiring. Training should be phased (park-funded initially, tenant co-funded subsequently).

---

## Medium-Term Opportunities (18–36 months)

**Regional Trade (ECOWAS/AfCFTA) Is Underexploited:**  
Border-proximate sites (Northern sites, Oxygen City) should develop regional buyer relationships and trade facilitation services. This is a secondary market driver, not immediate.

**Backward Integration (Cotton-to-Garment) Requires Separate Textile Sector Stabilization:**  
Parks should not position as integrated hubs unless paired with textile sector investment. Garment operators will continue regional sourcing in the near term.

**Trade Finance Facilitation Can Improve Tenant Cash Flow:**  
Parks should facilitate linkages to trade finance providers and advocate for FX retention policy. This reduces tenant financing cost and improves cash flow.

# Site Prioritization Logic

| Site Type | Competitive Advantage | Recommended Tenant Mix | Key Risks |
|----------|------------------------|-------------------------|-----------|
| **Tema-Proximate (Oxygen City, Accra-area)** | Port proximity, grid access, buyer networks, labor supply | Export-oriented (fast fashion, time-sensitive), government procurement | High land cost, congestion, competition from informal sector |
| **Northern Sites (Savelugu)** | Solar resource, lower land cost, regional trade access, domestic procurement | Government procurement (uniforms), regional trade, domestic-focused operators | Logistics disadvantage (4–6 hr to Tema), workforce recruitment cost, buyer access |

**Site sensitivity summary:**  
- Logistics-advantaged sites (Tema-proximate) should target export-oriented, time-sensitive tenants and justify shared compliance infrastructure.  
- Northern sites should target domestic procurement (government uniforms) and regional trade, with emphasis on soft infrastructure (compliance support, trade facilitation) to offset logistics disadvantage.

# Critical Gaps and Next-Stage Evidence Needs

**Quantitative Productivity and Cost Data:**  
Defect rates, rework costs, lead times, productivity (units/worker/day), operating costs ($/unit or % of revenue) by operator and product type.

**Land Readiness Assessment:**  
Land availability, title status, acquisition timeline, infrastructure status (utilities, roads, drainage) for each candidate site (Savelugu, Oxygen City, others).

**Workforce Skills Audit:**  
Skills assessment by role (pattern makers, quality inspectors, maintenance technicians, operators), wage expectations, training supply, training cost model.

**Buyer Relationship Data:**  
Number of buyers, order frequency, volume, lead times, seasonal demand patterns, buyer concentration risk by operator.

**Policy Barrier Assessment:**  
Specific regulatory or trade policy constraints (FX retention, customs processes, energy subsidies, labor regulations) and government support options.

**Regional Competitor Benchmarking:**  
Productivity, lead times, compliance, cost per unit in Côte d'Ivoire, Benin, Ethiopia to validate Ghana's competitive position.

**Captive Power Economics:**  
Capex and opex for solar + battery systems, payback period, tenant willingness to pay for energy cost savings.

**Trade Finance Access:**  
Availability and cost of trade finance (letters of credit, factoring) for garment operators; FX retention policy constraints.
